Recording Your Course Content

Once you have chosen your topic, created your course outline, and planned your content it's important to start planning your video production. Don't worry if you've never recorded an online course. We have resources for you along the way!

Step 1: Determine the type of videos you'll record

Once your course content is planned you have validated your course topic and completed your course outline. It's time to record. You can record yourself talking (talking head), record your screen (screencasting), or do a mix of both. The type of course you'll record will determine the equipment you'll need.

Step 2: Gather the audio and video equipment you need

You may even already have some equipment at home! Have a look at our recording equipment recommendations for suggestions for microphones, cameras, and screencasting and calling software for various budgets.

Step 3: Set up your recording space

Once you have your recording equipment it's time to find a quiet place to set it up and get ready to record. Follow our guide for setting up your recording space to deliver high quality audio and videos, plus get tips on screen recording (look at other instructors' recording setups for inspiration).

Step 4: Send us a test Video

Once your recording space is set up, it's time to test it out! We have a team ready to give you feedback to ensure you meet our recording quality standards. Learn more about submitting a test video.

Step 5: Edit your course

After you've completed recording your course content, it's time to edit your videos before uploading to Udemy. Consider taking a course on the editing process or learn from some of our experienced instructors by taking the Official Udemy Course: "How to Create an Online Course".

FAQs

How do I decide what type of recording I'll need to do for my course?

Let your course content be your guide. Based on your content you should pick the video format that allows for the best visual accompaniment for your message. If you're teaching coding or a technical course, you'll most likely record your screen while you talk (called "screencasting"). If you don't need to show slides or a screen to demonstrate what you're teaching, "talking head" videos are typically most effective. You may even want to show slides with an audio overlay, which is great when you need students to visualize complex concepts.

How much money do I need to spend on equipment?

This depends on what equipment you may already have and how much you'd like to invest. Most smartphones can capture excellent video. You might consider using an external microphone as smartphone built-in microphones typically don't capture the best audio. Check our recommendations to see what you already own and experiment with your setup before investing in new equipment.

How does the test video process work?

Once your recording space is set up, you can submit a 5 minute test video to get personalized feedback on your recording quality. Once your test video is received, our team will provide you with feedback on the quality of your audio, video, and display. While this is not a requirement, it's highly recommended so that you can feel confident that your recording quality meets our standards. If you change your recording setup at any time, you can submit another test video.
